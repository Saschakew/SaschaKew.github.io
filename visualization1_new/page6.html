$T$ is the number of observations used to compute the sample statistics and plots.

Rotation angle: $\phi$

The innovations are computed as $e_t(B(\phi)) = B(\phi)^{-1} u_t$ where $B(\phi)$ is a rotation of the true mixing matrix. Varying $\phi$ rotates the reduced-form shocks $u_t$ and changes the higher moments of $e_t$.

×

True rotation angle: $\phi_0$

$\phi_0$ denotes the true rotation used to construct the reduced-form shocks. It is fixed on this page and shown for reference.

×

Mixture shift: $s$

$s$ shifts one component of the mixture distribution and controls the degree and direction of non-Gaussianity (skewness) of $\epsilon_{1,t}$.

Impact of Non-Gaussianity on Estimation

This page illustrates how the degree of non-Gaussianity affects the estimation of structural shocks in our SVAR model.

Shock Distributions

In the simulation below, the second structural form shock $\epsilon_{2,t}$ is independently drawn from a uniform distribution. The first structural form shock $\epsilon_{1,t}$ is independently drawn from a mixture of normal distributions i

$$ \epsilon_{1,t} \sim 0.9 \mathcal{N} (0, 1 ) + 0.1 \mathcal{N} (s, 1). $$

The mixing coefficient $s$ can be chosen using the slider above:

  • For $s = 0$, both shocks are normal.
  • For $s < 0$, the first shock is left-skewed.
  • For $s > 0$, the first shock is right-skewed.

Interactive Simulation

Move the $\phi$ and $s$ sliders to see how the rotations and non-Gaussianity affect the dependencies of the innovations displayed in the table below the scatter plot.

Interactive Loss Calculation

The loss value in the table again sums up all squared co-moments to get an overall measure of the dependencies

$$ loss(\phi) = \text{mean}( e_{1,t}(\phi) e_{2,t}(\phi) )^2 + \; .... \; + \text{mean}( e_{1,t}(\phi)^2 e_{2,t}(\phi)^2 -1)^2 $$

The plot below shows the loss for all rotation angles $\phi$.

  • Push the Minimize-Dependencies button to find the rotation angle $\phi$ which leads to the least dependent innovations.
  • Vary the degree of non-Gaussianity using $s$ to see how it affects the optimization landscape.

Take-away: Gaussian shocks lead to a flat optimization landscape without a unique minimum at $\phi_0$. This illustrates why non-Gaussianity is crucial for identifying structural shocks in SVAR models based on independent shocks.
