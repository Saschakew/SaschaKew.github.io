This last pages illustrates how the degree of non-Gaussianity affects the estimation.

In the simulation below, the second structural form shock $\epsilon_{2,t}$ is independently drawn from a uniform distribution. The first structural form shock $\epsilon_{1,t}$ is independently drawn from a mixture of uniform distributionsi $$ \epsilon_{1,t} \sim 0.9 \mathcal{N} (0, 1 ) + 0.1 \mathcal{N} (s, 1) $$ where the mixing coefficient $s$ can be chosen using the slider above. For $s=0$, both shocks are normal. For $s < 0$ the first shock is left skewed and for $s > 0$ the first shock is right skewed.

Move the $\phi$ and $s$ slider to see how the rotations and non-Gaussianity affects the dependencies of the innovations displayed in the table below the scatter plot.

The loss value in the table again summes up all squared co-moments to get an overall measure of the dependencies $$ loss(\phi) = mean( e_{1,t}(\phi) e_{2,t}(\phi) )^2 + .... + mean( e_{1,t}(\phi)^2 e_{2,t}(\phi)^2 -1)^2 $$ The plot below shows how the loss for all rotation angles $\phi$.
Push the Minimize-Dependencies-Button to find the rotation angle $\phi$ which leads to the least dependent innovations.
Vary the degree of non-Gaussianity using $s$ to see it affects the optimization landscape.

Take-away: Gaussian shocks lead to a flat optimization landscape without a unique minimum at $\phi_0$.
